What is "cash to close"?

It is the total cash you need on closing day — your down payment plus land transfer tax plus the one-time costs to complete the purchase. It is separate from your monthly mortgage payment.

Why it matters

Buyers often save for the down payment but forget the closing costs. On a typical GTA purchase these can add tens of thousands of dollars. Knowing the full number before you make an offer prevents a nasty surprise at the lawyer's office.

When is it due?

Cash to close is paid on your closing day, usually through your lawyer. You'll typically provide it a day or two before, by certified cheque or wire. Unlike the CMHC premium, these costs cannot be rolled into your mortgage — they must be paid in cash.

Can I reduce my cash to close?

A few ways: a larger down payment lowers your CMHC premium and its PST; first-time buyers can claim the land transfer tax rebate; and some lenders offer cash-back products. The biggest lever is choosing a price and location that keeps land transfer tax manageable — a home outside Toronto avoids the second municipal tax entirely.

What buyers usually miss

A mortgage payment is not the full cost of owning the home. Property tax, insurance, utilities, condo fees and repairs can change the real monthly number quickly.

When to call

If the payment looks comfortable but the down payment is under 20 percent, check the CMHC calculator too. The premium can change the mortgage amount.

💡 Did you know?

On a 25-year mortgage, the first few years of payments go mostly to interest, not principal. It can take roughly 5 years before a meaningful chunk starts paying down the loan itself.

💡 Did you know?

Switching from monthly to accelerated bi-weekly payments makes the equivalent of one extra monthly payment a year — and can shave years off your mortgage and save tens of thousands in interest.

Mortgage calculator — common questions

Does the mortgage payment estimate include property tax and utilities?

No. The mortgage line estimates principal and interest only. Property tax, insurance, utilities, condo fees and repairs should be reviewed separately before you decide whether the home is comfortable.

Why is my bank's payment different from this calculator?

Your lender may use a different rate, payment frequency, compounding assumption, insurance premium, amortization or rounding. Use this as a planning estimate, then confirm the final payment with your lender or mortgage broker.

Should I compare more than one down payment scenario?

Yes. A different down payment can change the CMHC premium, total mortgage and monthly payment. That is why the payment tab shows four side-by-side scenarios.

Should I calculate payment or closing costs first?

Do both before touring. Payment tells you whether the home is comfortable monthly. Closing costs tell you whether you have enough cash to complete the purchase.

Why does CMHC change the mortgage amount?

When the down payment is under 20 percent, the insurance premium is often added to the mortgage. That means you may borrow more than the purchase price minus your down payment.

What does amortization mean here?

Amortization is the full number of years used to pay the mortgage down to zero if the payment schedule and rate stayed as entered. A longer amortization usually lowers the payment but increases total interest.

What does payment frequency change?

Frequency changes how often payments are made. Monthly, weekly and bi-weekly payments can have different per-payment amounts, and accelerated options can pay the loan down faster.

Why does the interest rate matter so much?

The rate changes both the payment and the speed at which principal is paid down. Even a small rate difference can add up when the mortgage is large or the amortization is long.

Can this calculator tell me what I qualify for?

No. It helps you test comfort and compare scenarios. A lender or mortgage broker still has to review income, debts, credit, documents and mortgage rules before confirming approval.

Should I use the lowest payment I can find?

Not by itself. A low payment can still leave you tight after property tax, insurance, utilities, condo fees, repairs and savings. Use the number as a starting point, then stress-test the real monthly cost.

How much income do I need to afford this payment?

Lenders look at your total housing costs against your income (the gross debt service ratio, usually kept around 39 percent) and all your debts together (the total debt service ratio, usually around 44 percent). A rough guide: a $4,800 monthly payment often needs a household income in the $175,000 range, but your exact number depends on property tax, heat, condo fees and other debts. A mortgage broker can confirm your real qualifying amount. Read: pre-approval vs your real budget →

Should I pick a fixed or variable rate?

Fixed gives you payment certainty for the term. Variable can be cheaper but moves with the Bank of Canada. The right choice depends on your budget, how much payment change you can handle, and where rates are heading. Read: fixed vs variable in 2026 →

Where can I get my down payment from?

Common sources include your savings, a gift from an immediate family member, your RRSP through the Home Buyers' Plan, and an FHSA. Each has its own rules and proof requirements. Read: down payment sources →  |  FHSA vs RRSP →

Do Brampton and Mississauga buyers pay Toronto land transfer tax?

No. They usually pay Ontario land transfer tax only, unless the property is inside the City of Toronto boundary. The calculator now reads the location field and only applies Toronto MLTT for Toronto-area locations.

When does Toronto MLTT apply?

Toronto MLTT applies when the property is inside the City of Toronto. Toronto, North York, Scarborough, Etobicoke, East York and York are treated as Toronto areas here.

Does the first-time buyer rebate remove all land transfer tax?

Not always. Ontario has a rebate up to $4,000 and Toronto has a rebate up to $4,475. On many GTA prices, the rebate helps but does not remove the full tax.

How is land transfer tax calculated manually?

The purchase price is split into tax brackets. Each slice is taxed at its own rate, then eligible first-time buyer rebates are deducted. The Land Transfer Tax tab shows the bracket breakdown so a buyer can follow the math.

Who confirms the final land transfer tax before closing?

Your real estate lawyer confirms the final amount. This calculator is useful for planning, but your lawyer should confirm exact tax, rebate eligibility and closing adjustments.

Why do some Toronto purchases show two taxes?

Toronto properties can have Ontario land transfer tax and Toronto municipal land transfer tax. The municipal tax is separate, so the closing number can be much higher than a similar property outside Toronto.

What happens when I type Scarborough, North York or Etobicoke?

The calculator treats those locations as part of the City of Toronto for this estimate. It will include Ontario LTT and Toronto MLTT and show a note under the location row.

Why does the rebate appear in my calculation?

The rebate appears only when First-time homebuyer is set to Yes and the residency selection supports the estimate. The note is there so the buyer knows the lower tax depends on that assumption.

Do non-resident taxes apply to every buyer?

No. Non-resident speculation tax (NRST) only applies to foreign buyers and depends on status, property type, location and current rules. It is shown separately and only when it applies — a lawyer or tax professional should confirm your exact situation.

Can the lawyer's final number differ from this estimate?

Yes. Your lawyer may include closing adjustments, title insurance, registration costs, rebates, exemptions or rule details that are outside this quick calculator. Use this for planning, not as the final statement of adjustments. Read: closing costs in Ontario →

When is CMHC mortgage insurance required?

CMHC mortgage insurance is usually required when the down payment is less than 20 percent on an eligible insured mortgage.

Is CMHC insurance paid upfront?

The premium is usually added to the mortgage instead of being paid fully upfront. In Ontario, sales tax on the premium may be a cash closing cost.

What happens if the purchase price is $1.5 million or more?

Insured mortgage rules generally do not apply at $1.5 million and above. Buyers usually need an uninsured mortgage and a larger down payment.

Can I get a 30-year amortization with less than 20 percent down?

Insured 30-year amortization rules can depend on borrower type and property type. Confirm carefully with your mortgage broker.

Is CMHC the only mortgage default insurance provider?

No. CMHC is one provider. Other mortgage default insurance providers include Sagen, formerly Genworth Canada, and Canada Guaranty.

Why is the premium $0 at 20 percent down?

At 20 percent down or more, a mortgage is usually uninsured by default, so this calculator shows no CMHC premium. If you put down less than the legal minimum (5 percent on the first $500,000, 10 percent on the portion above), the calculator will tell you the down payment is too low rather than showing a premium.

What is the minimum down payment in Canada?

5 percent on the first $500,000 of the price, plus 10 percent on any portion between $500,000 and $1.5 million. At $1.5 million and above you generally need 20 percent. For example, on a $950,000 home the minimum is $25,000 + $45,000 = $70,000.

How does loan-to-value affect the premium?

Loan-to-value compares the mortgage before insurance with the purchase price. A higher loan-to-value usually means a higher premium rate because the down payment is smaller.

Does CMHC approve my mortgage?

CMHC insurance is only one part of the lending process. The lender still reviews income, debt, credit, property details and documents before giving final mortgage approval.

Why does CMHC affect the payment calculator?

If the premium is added to the mortgage, the payment is calculated on the larger insured mortgage amount. That is why the payment can change even when the purchase price and down payment look unchanged.

Do I pay sales tax on the insurance premium?

In Ontario, provincial sales tax can apply to the mortgage insurance premium and may be due as part of closing costs. Confirm the exact amount with your lawyer or lender before closing. Buying a condo? Read about maintenance fees →

What is "cash to close"?

It is the total cash you need on closing day: your down payment plus land transfer tax plus the one-time costs to complete the purchase (legal fees, title insurance, inspection and so on). It is separate from your monthly mortgage payment.

How much should I budget for closing costs?

CMHC suggests budgeting 1.5% to 4% of the purchase price for closing costs. On a $950,000 home that is roughly $14,000 to $38,000 on top of your down payment, depending on legal fees, location and whether mortgage insurance applies. Read: closing costs in Ontario →

Are closing costs separate from the down payment?

Yes. The down payment goes toward the purchase price. Closing costs are additional one-time expenses to complete the transaction. You need both amounts in cash on closing day.

What is the adjustments buffer?

It is a rough allowance (here, 0.25% of the price) for closing adjustments your lawyer settles with the seller, such as prepaid property taxes or utilities. Your lawyer's statement of adjustments confirms the exact figures.

Do I pay land transfer tax in cash at closing?

Yes. Land transfer tax is paid at closing and cannot be added to the mortgage. In Toronto there are two land transfer taxes (Ontario plus municipal), which is why the cash needed there is higher.

Can these closing costs change?

Yes. These are planning estimates. Legal fees vary by firm, title insurance varies by property, and your lawyer may add registration fees or other adjustments. Always confirm the final number with your lawyer before closing. Read: closing costs in Ontario →
